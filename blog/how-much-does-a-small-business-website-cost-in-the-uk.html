How Much Does a Small Business Website Cost in the UK?

A practical guide to typical website costs, what affects pricing and how to budget sensibly for your business.

← Back to Blog Published 13 June 2026

If you run a small business in the UK and need a new website, one of the first questions you’ll ask is: how much will it cost? It’s a fair question — and one that many business owners find frustrating, because website pricing can feel unclear, inconsistent and difficult to compare.

Quotes can vary wildly depending on who you speak to, what’s included and how complex your requirements are. That uncertainty often makes it harder to budget confidently or know whether you’re getting good value.

The honest answer is that it depends on what you need, who builds it and how much ongoing support you want afterwards. This guide explains typical price ranges, what affects the final cost and what you should budget beyond the initial build.

Typical UK website cost ranges

Most small business websites in the UK fall into one of the following brackets:

  • DIY website builders: often £10–£40 per month, plus your time to set up content, pages and basic SEO.
  • Template-based or budget freelancer sites: roughly £300–£800 for a simple brochure-style website with limited customisation.
  • Professional small business websites: commonly £750–£2,500 for a properly designed, mobile-friendly site with contact forms, SEO foundations and business-focused structure.
  • More advanced business websites: £2,500–£5,000+ where you need extra pages, blog sections, landing pages, integrations, booking tools or more bespoke functionality.

These figures are guides, not fixed rules. A very simple one-page site will cost less. A larger site with custom features, copywriting, photography or e-commerce will cost more.

What affects the price of a website?

Several factors influence how much you’ll pay:

  • Number of pages: a 5-page brochure site is very different from a 20-page site with service areas, case studies and blog posts.
  • Design quality: custom design takes longer than adapting a basic template and usually produces a more professional result.
  • Content: if you supply ready-written text and images, costs are lower. If the developer or an agency needs to create content for you, the price increases.
  • Functionality: contact forms are standard, but booking systems, CRM integrations, member areas or e-commerce add complexity and cost.
  • SEO setup: basic on-page SEO should be included in a professional build, but ongoing SEO work is usually priced separately.
  • Revisions and project management: clear requirements and timely feedback help keep costs under control.

One-off costs vs ongoing costs

It helps to think about website spending in two parts: the build and the ongoing running costs.

One-off project costs usually include design, development, setup of contact forms, mobile responsiveness, basic SEO configuration, analytics setup and launch support.

Ongoing costs may include:

  • Domain name: often around £10–£20 per year.
  • Hosting: from a few pounds per month on basic hosting to £25+ per month for managed business hosting with support and backups.
  • Maintenance: updates, security checks, small content changes and technical support, often billed monthly if you want peace of mind.
  • SEO and marketing: optional, but valuable if you want to improve visibility on Google over time.

A low upfront website price can become more expensive later if hosting, support and updates are not planned properly.

Why cheap websites can cost more

Choosing the lowest price can seem sensible, especially for a small business watching costs carefully. But a cheap website is not always a good deal if it fails to generate enquiries, looks unprofessional or needs replacing within a year or two.

A business owner spends £300 on a template website. Six months later they’re paying again because the site isn’t generating enquiries, loads slowly on mobile devices and no longer reflects the business properly. What looked cheap initially often ends up costing more in the long run.

Common problems with very low-cost websites include poor mobile performance, weak SEO foundations, limited support, generic design and functionality that doesn’t match how the business actually works. When that happens, you may end up paying again for redesigns, fixes or a completely new website.

What should a small business budget?

For most UK small businesses, a sensible approach is:

  • Budget for a professional website build that matches the size and goals of the business.
  • Allow for a domain and hosting from day one.
  • Decide whether you want to manage updates yourself or pay for ongoing maintenance.
  • Avoid choosing a website based only on the lowest price if enquiries, credibility and search visibility matter to your business.

A well-built website should help you look credible, explain what you do clearly and make it easy for customers to get in touch. That is often worth more than saving a few hundred pounds upfront on a weak design.

There is no single price that suits every small business website in the UK. A basic online presence costs far less than a bespoke, marketing-focused website with advanced features and ongoing SEO support.
